\section{Introduction and Positioning}
\label{sec:introduction}

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models and robot foundation models are moving robot autonomy from task-specific perception--planning--control stacks toward policies that jointly interpret images, language, robot state, and interaction history before producing actions. PaLM-E, RT-1, RT-2, VIMA, Open X-Embodiment, OpenVLA, Octo, and $\pi_0$ illustrate this shift toward web-scale multimodal pretraining, large robot datasets, cross-embodiment transfer, and natural-language task specification \cite{driess2023palme,brohan2022rt1,brohan2023rt2,jiang2023vima,openx2023,kim24openvla,ghosh2024octo,black2024pi0}. Related systems such as SayCan, Code as Policies, ProgPrompt, VoxPoser, CLIPort, Diffusion Policy, and ACT show that language and vision can also supervise skill selection, code generation, waypoint synthesis, trajectory distributions, and action-chunk prediction \cite{ahn2022saycan,liang2023code,singh2023progprompt,huang2023voxposer,shridhar2022cliport,chi2023diffusion,zhao2023act}. For robotics, this is not only a new model family. It is a new control interface whose inputs are semantically rich and whose outputs can move robots, operate tools, write memory, and change the future observation stream.

This interface makes attacks qualitatively different from ordinary VLM, LLM, or robot-policy failures. In a VLM, an adversarial image may yield a wrong answer; in a text-only LLM, a jailbreak may yield unsafe text; in a conventional robot policy, a disturbance may reduce control performance. In a VLA-enabled robot, these effects can meet inside a closed-loop embodied process. A printed patch can change visual grounding, a small instruction edit can redirect a trajectory, a scene-text injection can cross a source boundary, a poisoned demonstration can implant a trigger, and an action-level backdoor can force a gripper primitive at a critical moment. The consequence may appear as a wrong object, unauthorized tool use, action freezing, contact risk, privacy leakage, or persistent state compromise rather than as a single incorrect label.

\subsection{Related Surveys and Positioning}
\label{subsec:positioning}

The closest adjacent work is the VLA safety survey \emph{Vision-Language-Action Safety: Threats, Challenges, Evaluations, and Mechanisms} \cite{li2026vlasafety}. That survey covers the broader safety landscape: threats, alignment, evaluation, defense mechanisms, and deployment challenges. Our paper does not try to re-cover that landscape with a different emphasis. It asks a different set of review questions: \emph{Which attacks have actually been demonstrated against VLA models or VLA-controlled robots? What evidence level supports each claim? Which target representation was changed? How was attack success defined? Which results are direct VLA evidence, which are embodied-adjacent, and which are only analogical?}

This distinction matters because the literature is now moving quickly from position statements to concrete VLA attack papers. Direct evidence includes adversarial prompt and patch attacks on VLA policies \cite{jones2025robogcg,wang2025vlaadversarial,yan2025vlafool,wu2026saber}, action freezing and action/trajectory backdoors \cite{wang2025freezevla,zhou2025badvla,zhou2025goba,xu2025dropvla,li2025attackvla,xu2026silentdrift}, transferable and model-agnostic patch attacks \cite{xu2025edpa,lu2026patch}, 3D object-texture attacks \cite{chen2026tex3d}, CoT-reasoning hijacking \cite{huang2026trap}, trajectory redirection \cite{puthumanaillam2026trajectory}, and VLA membership inference \cite{peng2026miavla,luan2026vlaleaks}. These papers are not just examples inside a safety taxonomy; they define the empirical base that an authoritative attack survey must compare.

\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{subsec:rqs}

The review is organized around five research questions.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Which attacks have been validated directly on VLA models, reasoning-enabled VLAs, VLA-controlled robots, or VLA training/privacy channels?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} Which lifecycle stages, entry surfaces, target components, target representations, and action representations do these attacks affect?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:} What knowledge level, access channel, and privilege level do current attacks assume?
    \item \textbf{RQ4:} What validation tier do current studies reach, and do they report real actuation, controller behavior, safety filters, or recovery?
    \item \textbf{RQ5:} Where do current evaluations remain weak in comparability, transfer, physical validation, and robotics safety interpretation?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scope and Definition}

We treat a \emph{VLA attack} as an intentional intervention that changes a VLA-enabled system's perception, grounding, planning, policy decoding, execution, memory, tool use, privacy boundary, or human-robot interaction in a way that violates the intended task, authorization boundary, privacy expectation, or safety envelope. This definition separates adversarial attacks from ordinary task failure and non-adversarial robustness failure. A robot that fails because of unmodeled friction exhibits robustness risk; a robot that fails because an adversary selected a perturbation, instruction, tool response, memory entry, trigger, or physical scene configuration to induce a violation exhibits security risk.

We include three evidence strata. \emph{Direct evidence} attacks VLA models or VLA-controlled robots. \emph{Embodied-adjacent evidence} attacks embodied LLM/VLM planners, robot policies, middleware, or HRI systems that share action or physical consequence boundaries but are not end-to-end VLA policies. \emph{Analogical evidence} comes from adversarial classifiers, autonomous sensing, software agents, RAG, general RL, or robot cybersecurity when it informs a VLA attack surface but does not by itself establish a VLA result. We exclude ordinary distribution shift without an adversarial objective, purely text-only jailbreak work without an embodied implication, and image-classification attacks that do not help analyze grounding, planning, action, privacy, or physical consequence.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper discusses attacks at a responsible academic level and does not provide operational payloads, exploit recipes, or instructions for real-world misuse. It makes five contributions.

\begin{itemize}
    \item We reposition the paper as a systematic evidence review of attacks on VLA-enabled robotic systems, with explicit contrast to VLA safety and embodied-AI surveys.
    \item We provide a transparent evidence base that separates direct VLA attacks, embodied-adjacent attacks, and contextual references without letting background work inflate attack claims.
    \item We formalize typed VLA attack success, separating task failure, targeted behavior, safety violation, authorization violation, privacy leakage, persistence, and recovery failure.
    \item We replace mixed one-dimensional categories with an orthogonal attack map covering lifecycle, entry surface, target component, target representation, security property, attacker assumptions, validation tier, and consequence.
    \item We synthesize direct VLA attacks through their mechanisms and robotic consequences, including cross-study findings on target models, validation, action representations, controller reporting, and recovery gaps.
    \item We propose a standardized evaluation protocol for VLA attacks that connects typed ASR, benign utility, transfer, query budget, physical validation, closed-loop recovery, and consequence severity.
\end{itemize}
