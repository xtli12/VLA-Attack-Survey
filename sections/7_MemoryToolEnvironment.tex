\section{Open Problems}
\label{sec:open-problems}

\subsection{From Direct Evidence to General Evidence}

Direct VLA attack evidence is no longer absent, but it is not yet broad enough for field-wide conclusions. The next step is replication across action-token, chunked, diffusion, flow, waypoint, skill-based, and hierarchical VLA systems; across manipulation, navigation, mobile manipulation, and human-proximate settings; and across controllers and safety filters. A result on OpenVLA/LIBERO should be reported as important direct evidence, not as proof of universal VLA vulnerability.

\subsection{Physical Recovery and HIL Protocols}

The missing middle tier is hardware-in-the-loop. Simulation gives scale, and physical robots give realism, but HIL can expose real sensors, middleware timing, safety filters, and controller latency without requiring unsafe full deployment. Standard HIL protocols should record when an attack first changes representation, whether a controller clips or rejects it, whether the robot recovers after new observations, and whether human intervention changes the outcome.

\subsection{Action-Representation Transfer}

The field needs attacks and defenses that test transfer across action representations, not only across model names. A prompt attack may transfer across language-conditioned token policies but fail against a flow policy; a patch may transfer across visual encoders but not across camera placements; a chunk-drift backdoor may be irrelevant to a one-step replanner. Evaluation suites should vary tokenizer, chunk length, temporal ensemble, diffusion/flow sampler, waypoint controller, and low-level safety envelope.

\subsection{Memory, Tool, Middleware, and HRI}

Direct VLA evidence is still thin for memory poisoning, tool/API misuse, middleware compromise, and socially situated attackers. Adjacent evidence from RAG, software agents, ROS/ROS2, and HRI shows credible mechanisms \cite{zou2025poisonedrag,debenedetti2024agentdojo,deng2022insecurityros2,booth2017piggybacking}, but VLA attack studies need to measure robot consequences: unauthorized skill use, corrupted maps, persistent user preference changes, unsafe tool arguments, privacy leakage, and recovery after correction.

\subsection{Privacy as a First-Class VLA Attack Objective}

Membership inference work shows that VLA actions and trajectories can leak training membership \cite{peng2026miavla,luan2026vlaleaks}. Future privacy evaluation should extend beyond membership to property inference, dataset provenance, user routine leakage, private-scene memorization, and cross-session log leakage. These are direct VLA security questions even when no robot collision or task failure occurs.

\subsection{Adaptive Attacks and Responsible Artifacts}

First-generation defenses will use prompt hierarchy, source labels, retrieval filters, tool permissions, runtime monitors, action shields, control barrier functions, and human approvals. Attack research must therefore include adaptive settings: what the attacker knows, what feedback is available, whether queries are budgeted, and whether benign utility is preserved. At the same time, VLA attack artifacts can be unusually transferable to physical systems. Benchmarks should separate public scoring code and sanitized examples from high-risk payloads, physical exploit details, or deployment-specific trigger recipes.

\subsection{Limitations of This Review}

This review freezes the evidence database at the 16 June 2026 search date, so later arXiv releases, renamed project pages, and venue updates are outside scope. Google Scholar and Semantic Scholar ranking can bias discovery toward highly visible papers, while targeted-name searches can miss attacks that use different terminology. We searched English-language sources and may miss non-English reports. Several direct VLA attacks are recent preprints or workshop papers; their claims may change after peer review, and project-page or repository information is weaker evidence than archival text.

The complete database is auditable, but it records what papers report, not independent reproduction of their experiments. The independent second audit covers all 23 direct VLA records and principal fields, plus a stratified sample of non-direct records, but it does not re-run attacks, inspect all source code, or verify every ASR number. Coding remains partly judgment-based, especially for multi-label target representations, transfer reporting, and validation flags. Finally, the descriptive statistics are counts and proportions over a small database, not a meta-analysis. We do not pool ASR values across attack objectives, denominators, validation tiers, or robot platforms, and multi-label percentages can change noticeably when a single new direct VLA paper is added.
